## What this rank means



Second Class shows that the scout is moving from beginner to active participant. They have spent more time practicing outdoor skills, service, fitness, safety, and troop participation.

A Second Class scout should be gaining confidence and starting to see how repeated practice builds competence.



## Their role in the troop and patrol model



At this stage, the scout should be a more dependable patrol member. They should understand how patrols organize work, how youth leaders guide the troop, and how scouts support each other on outings.

They may still need reminders, but they should be contributing more consistently.



## What they worked on



The scout has been building outdoor confidence, safety awareness, fitness habits, service experience, citizenship awareness, and practical skills. They have likely had more chances to learn from mistakes on outings and in patrol activities.



## How they should think about the next rank



First Class is a major milestone. Moving toward First Class means becoming a self-sufficient scout who can camp, cook, navigate, serve, and participate with less adult direction. The scout should start thinking about how they can help newer scouts learn what they have learned.



## Questions parents can ask



- What skill do you feel much more confident doing now than when you joined?
- What did you learn from a campout or outing that did not go perfectly?
- How do you prepare differently now than you did as a new scout?
- What does your patrol count on you to do?
- What service activity was meaningful to you, and why?
- How have you grown in fitness, responsibility, or confidence?
- What is one thing you learned from an older scout?
- What is one thing you could teach a newer scout?
- What part of First Class feels most challenging?
- How can the troop make outings better for scouts your age?



## Parent reviewer reminder



Listen for increasing independence. The scout does not need to sound like a senior leader yet.
