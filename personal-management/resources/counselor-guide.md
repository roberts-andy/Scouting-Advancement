# Counselor Guide

## Teaching stance

Personal Management is an experience-driven badge. Your job is to launch the process clearly, keep scouts honest, and help them reflect on what their real data shows.

## What good work looks like

- The scout tracks real money over 13 consecutive weeks.
- Entries have dates, categories, amounts, and enough notes to explain them later.
- The budget is a plan, the log is reality, and the final comparison explains the gap.
- The scout can explain tradeoffs, not just hand over a worksheet.

## What to avoid

- A single long lecture with immediate sign-off.
- Accepting a completed spreadsheet without discussion.
- Letting scouts compress or backfill the 13-week period.
- Treating perfect spending as the goal. Honest tracking and reflection are the goal.

## Requirement-area coaching approach

| Area | How to teach it | What to listen for |
|---|---|---|
| Major purchase and savings | Treat it like a mini project with options, pricing, timeline, and tradeoffs. | Scout can explain why the item matters and what they would give up to afford it. |
| 13-week budget | Teach budget as plan, tracking as reality, comparison as learning. | Scout has consistent entries and can explain variances. |
| Discussion topics | Use guided conversation and real examples. | Scout can apply concepts to their own decisions. |
| Saving/investing/interest/risk | Use simple analogies and avoid jargon-first teaching. | Scout understands safety, growth, risk, time, and diversification at a practical level. |
| Insurance and risk management | Frame as paying to avoid a financial catastrophe. | Scout can describe why people insure low-probability, high-impact risks. |

## Red flags in tracking logs

- Large gaps followed by many entries on one day.
- Round numbers for every transaction.
- No small purchases.
- No income or allowance sources when the scout says they spent money.
- No variance between planned and actual amounts.
- Parent appears to have completed the work for the scout.

## Final review standard

You are validating understanding. A strong final review sounds like the scout saying: "Here is what I planned, here is what actually happened, here is why it was different, and here is what I would do differently next time."
